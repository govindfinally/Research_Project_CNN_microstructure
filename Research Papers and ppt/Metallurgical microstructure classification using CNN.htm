Abstract

Predicting metallurgical microstructures is challenging due to the intricate features that emerge during manufacturing. This research utilizes Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) to recognize microstructure images of mild steel alloys, representing diverse microstructures. The study focuses on forecasting steel microstructure properties, specifically identifying and predicting Pearlite (P) and Bainite (B) microstructures after steel heat treatment, using a CNN approach. This comprehensive workflow leads to robust microstructure classification, achieving a model training accuracy rate of 98.95Â %. Introduction

Predicting metallurgical microstructures is inherently challenging due to the complex structures and diverse features that develop during the metallurgical processes. Study incorporates the use of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) to recognize microstructure images of alloys of Aluminum (Al) and Iron (Fe), based on their various morphological structures, including spheres, petals, needles, and dendrites. The CNN achieved an impressive accuracy rate of 94.6Â % [1]. Recently, several metal manufacturing industries have expressed interest in use of computer vision and image processing into their operations. This technology enables the cost-effective design of materials to achieve desired qualities. Microstructure modeling can be particularly valuable in implementing various processes, including heat treatment. Study focuses on predicting the microstructure properties of phases during the heat treatment of steels [2]. Moreover an automated microstructure algorithm utilized to assess the quality of materials. In some cases, computational image processing techniques have proven to be more effective than traditional methods for microstructures [3]. The study predicts that microstructure depends on mechanical properties in additively manufactured SS316L using a CNN algorithm. Among all the data-driven models tested, the CNN provides the best prediction with minimal preprocessing time. The study also provides a summary of current research on various aspects of phase transformation in steel and its improved properties. To explore diffusion-controlled phase transformation in steel and the kinetics of austenitization in pearlite and ferrite transformation in low-carbon steel. Identifying such complex phase transformations is a challenging task that requires expertise to understanding microstructures accurately [4], [5]. Additionally an adaptive characterization of a titanium alloy (Ti-6Al-4Â V) microstructure dataset was conducted using a two-stage machine learning approach. The study employed quantitative data from micrographs for microstructure feature recognition using a CNN algorithm. It utilized a dataset comprising 1225 images of the alloy and reported an accuracy of approximately 93Â % with a margin ofÂ Â±Â 1.17Â % [6]. Furthermore pattern recognition of optical microscopic images of steel was performed using the random forest statistical algorithm technique. The author compared the accuracy of microstructure percentage with linear analysis [7]. Additionally, the carbon content present in iron (Fe) influences the categorization of steel into three significant types: low carbon, medium carbon, and high carbon steel. These distinctions are determined by various heat treatments such as annealing, normalizing, tempering, and hardening, which induce different microstructures at diverse temperatures and cooling rates. These microstructures, including Martensite (M), Pearlite (P), Bainite (B), and Spheroidite (S), are observable in the Time-Temperature-Transformation (TTT) diagram [8]. Depending on the cooling rates, steel can exhibit various microstructures, including Ferrite (F), Pearlite (P), Bainite (B), and Martensite (M). These transformations stem from both displacive and reconstructive processes within the Austenite crystal structure (A). Moreover, cementite precipitation occurs at varying diffusion rates, except in the case of Martensite (M). These transformations and precipitation patterns are depicted on the Continuous Cooling Transformation (CCT) diagram. The study employed a dataset comprising 1225 images, from which multiple morphological features were extracted using algorithmic operations. A Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) was trained using the Keras API, consisting of three layers to classify microstructures into three categories: laminar, duplex, and acicular. Data analysis focused on critical aspects such as phase identification, grain characterization, and defect detection, leveraging advanced image processing techniques including phase segmentation, Sobel, watershed, and region-based segmentation. Integration of CNN into the algorithmic approach enhanced its capabilities and improved the accuracy of microstructural analysis. In a Machine Learning (ML) approach, a dataset of 730 images was utilized for training purposes [9]. The study offers insights into computer vision and ML techniques for microstructural characterization and analysis, highlighting their importance in microstructural science. Traditionally, human judgment guided measurement and method selection, but recent technological advancements offer new avenues for extracting information from microstructures. CNNs, particularly in the context of metallurgical microstructures, are suggested for high-dimensional image representation, aiding in understanding the relationship between processing and microstructure properties [10]. Focused on GCr15 bearing steel, a high-carbon alloy enriched with chromium and manganese, the research analyzed 58 scanning electron microscope (SEM) images. The Mask R-CNN algorithm was employed to extract and analyze microstructural features, utilizing data from the COCO dataset, a recognized resource in computer vision and object detection tasks. This approach facilitates precise characterization and comprehension of the microstructure in high-quality GCr15 bearing steel alloy [11]. The study emphasizes the need for standardization of methods, enhancement of data quality and quantity, generalization of models, to explain of AI decisions, real-time analysis, multimodal data integration, and practical implementation of AI in industrial settings. To use CNN, a deep learning model, the research accurately categorizes various phases of microstructure, claiming high accuracy in microstructure classification [12]. In AI and ML the CNN stands for Convolutional Neural Network, a specialized type of deep learning algorithm designed for tasks such as image processing, computer vision, and pattern recognition, with particular proficiency in image classification and object detection [13]. The utilization of the TensorFlow library involves constructing and training CNN models to classify images into predefined classes. TensorFlow, in tandem with Keras, is a widely used open-source machine learning library developed by Google for building and training deep learning models [14], [15]. Researchers widely acknowledge the advantages of image processing in tasks such as identifying grain boundaries, grain sizes, and grain segmentation [16]. From the literature review, it was evident that many studies have concentrated on microstructure prediction for alloys like steel (SS316L), Ti, Fe-Ga, and high-ultra carbon, whereas very few have addressed microstructure prediction for low carbon plain steel. The literature also highlights the widespread recognition and acceptance of the CNN algorithm within the research community for microstructure prediction. This study introduces a novel approach to phase identification and prediction for low carbon steel microstructures using the CNN algorithm, bridging an existing research gap and presenting an opportunity for further advancement in the field of metallurgical microstructure analysis. The study proposes a phase identification technique to predict bainite and pearlite microstructure images, utilizing a total of 2508 images of mild steel.
